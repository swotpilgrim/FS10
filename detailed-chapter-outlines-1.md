# DETAILED CHAPTER OUTLINES: Appalachian Shadow

## Chapter 1: Mountain Store

Target Word Count: 1,700 words
POV: Ruth Harlan, first person
Setting: Company store, small West Virginia mining town
Time: Evening closing shift, late summer 1978

* Purpose: Establish Ruth's dissociative condition and working-class Appalachian setting through demonstration of her blackout episodes.

* Beats: Ruth closes the company store at 6 PM, locks up, goes through normal shutdown routine. Wakes up at 6 AM the next morning still in the store—inventory completely reorganized, books balanced, complicated stock rotation completed perfectly. She has zero memory of the night. Coworker arrives, mentions seeing her working "like a machine" through the windows at 2 AM. Ruth checks the work—it's flawless, professional, efficient. She's learned to live with these gaps in memory. Serves morning customers (mining families buying on credit), observes their economic desperation with detached competence. Receives telegram during shift: emergency contact number, formal code. She knows what it means—PROJECT SLEEPWALKER is activating her again.

* Dialogue Topics: Coworker commenting on Ruth's late-night work, customers discussing mine layoffs and family struggles, Ruth's brief phone call confirming she received message and will comply.

* Emotional Arc: Ruth starts with routine acceptance of her condition, shifts to unease when realizing she lost entire night, ends with controlled dread recognizing the telegram means another mission she won't remember.

* Key Details: 1970s company store atmosphere (shelves of overpriced goods, credit ledgers, handwritten inventory), coal country poverty visible in customers, Ruth's meticulous but emotionless reorganization work, telegram with coded language.

Time: Flashback to 1962, Sunday afternoon family gathering

* Dialogue Topics: Grandfather explaining family loyalty to young Ruth, relatives whispering about Ruth's episodes being hereditary, grandfather's proclamations about protecting family from government and outsiders.

* Emotional Arc: Young Ruth experiences terror during beating, confusion during dissociative episode, strange comfort from grandfather's attention. Adult Ruth narrating with bitter understanding of how abuse and protection were intertwined.

* Key Details: Mountain compound layout (main house, family cabins, gathering area), grandfather's physical presence and authority, 1960s Appalachian poverty, Ruth's first remembered dissociative episode, hard candy as token of twisted affection.

* Emotional Arc: Ruth enters meeting expecting mission briefing, experiences escalating horror as she learns extent of program's control over her, ends with feeling like property rather than partner.
